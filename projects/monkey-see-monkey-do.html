A robot that learns motor skills from a sentence of natural language.

Robotics LLMs Embedded Systems

Type "learn the jab punch." The system searches the web, reasons about the biomechanics, compiles a servo trajectory, and executes the motion on two custom 3-DOF arms. No pretraining, no reinforcement learning, no dataset. Hack the North 2025 Top 32, built in 36 hours.

1. Abstract

A zero-shot robotic skill-acquisition pipeline that maps a natural-language command to a precise servo sequence on two custom 3-DOF arms. The user types a sentence. The system searches the web for instructional content on the named motion, structures a motion guide with an LLM, compiles the guide into a synchronized bilateral servo trajectory, and executes it on hardware. End to end, command to motion, in 10 to 15 seconds.

The architecture is Kahneman's dual-system framing made into silicon. A deliberate System 2 reasoning layer (Cohere over Tavily web-search results) extracts anatomical landmarks from instructional text and emits a structured motion guide. A fast System 1 execution layer (custom skill compiler + ESP32 servo controller) turns that guide into precisely-timed actuator commands at 60 Hz. The two layers are separated cleanly: the LLM never touches a servo register, and the firmware never parses prose. 36 hours of hackathon time forced the discipline that makes the split feel deliberate rather than improvised.

The novelty is the pipeline, not the components. LLMs are commodity. Servos are commodity. The contribution is that the chain from "jab punch" to a 6-servo bilateral motion sequence is hardened against the two failure modes that kill text-to-motor systems: hallucinated motion guides and unsafe trajectories. Multi-stage filtering on the reasoning side cut invalid motion guides from roughly 40% to under 5%, and multi-layer safety validation on the execution side cut unsafe motions from roughly 15% to under 2%.

  • Top 32, Hack the North 2025. Built end to end in a 36-hour window, judged live on the floor.
  • 10 to 15 seconds from natural-language input to physical motion. Roughly 3 to 5 seconds search, 4 to 7 seconds LLM reasoning, 2 to 3 seconds compile and execute.
  • Two custom 3-DOF arms, six servos, bilateral synchronization. The build is dual-arm because most useful motor skills are.
  • Hallucination filtering 40% to under 5%. Multi-stage relevance scoring, anatomical landmark extraction, cross-source agreement, and feasibility check against the robot's kinematic envelope.
  • Unsafe-motion filtering 15% to under 2%. Joint limits, angular velocity caps, no-overlap zones between arms, and a simulation rehearsal pass before any servo command leaves the host.
  • Under 50 ms sync between visualisation and hardware, down from 2 to 3 seconds on the first naive implementation, via a timestamp-based command protocol with explicit acknowledgements.

2. Introduction

Teaching a robot a new motor skill traditionally costs thousands of examples and weeks of reinforcement learning. A human martial-arts student reads a description, watches a demonstration, and starts trying. The internet already contains careful descriptions of essentially every physical motion humans perform; the question is whether the same text can be piped directly into servo commands without an intermediate dataset.

  • The cost curve is the wrong shape. Reinforcement learning a single bimanual skill on a real robot is weeks of compute and bespoke reward engineering. Adding a second skill is most of that cost again.
  • The data already exists. Boxing manuals, dance instructionals, surgical-technique writeups, sign-language references, physical-therapy protocols. Each one is a structured description of a motion. None of them are training data in the conventional sense.
  • LLMs are now strong enough to bridge text and structure. The same models that can write code from prose can, with the right scaffolding, extract a sequence of joint configurations from a paragraph of instruction. The bottleneck is no longer linguistic.

2.1 Why dual-system, not a single LLM

The temptation is to ask the LLM to emit servo angles directly. That fails in two ways. The model hallucinates joint values that look reasonable in isolation but are kinematically invalid on the actual arm, and the prompt complexity needed to coerce production-grade output is brittle to small wording changes. Splitting the pipeline into a slow reasoning layer that emits a structured intermediate format, and a fast deterministic execution layer that compiles that format into commands, gives each layer the constraint surface it can hold.

  • The LLM emits structure, not commands. Output is a JSON motion guide with phases (preparation, execution, recovery), per-arm joint configurations, timing, velocity profiles, and safety metadata. The schema is the contract.
  • The compiler enforces physics. Inverse kinematics, trajectory smoothing, velocity profiling, and safety clamping all live downstream of the LLM. Anything the model says that violates the kinematic envelope gets caught before it reaches the firmware.
  • Failure modes are now separable. A hallucinated motion guide fails at the cross-source agreement step. An unsafe trajectory fails at the simulation rehearsal step. The two failure modes do not contaminate each other.

2.2 What is actually new

Cohere is off the shelf. Tavily is off the shelf. The ESP32 is off the shelf. The 3-DOF arm geometry is off the shelf. The contribution is the pipeline that holds those pieces together under the hackathon time budget and the two failure modes that text-to-motor systems usually die on.

  • Structured motion guides as the intermediate format. Phases, per-arm joints, timing, velocity, safety metadata. The schema is what lets the compiler do its job without re-prompting the LLM.
  • Multi-stage hallucination filtering. Relevance scoring on search results, anatomical landmark extraction, cross-source agreement across multiple instructional sources, and feasibility check against the robot's kinematic envelope. Each stage is small; the composition is what gets the 40% to under 5% number.
  • Synchronized bilateral execution. Two arms, six servos, time-aligned trajectories. The skill compiler emits both arms' commands in a single batched stream rather than two independent streams, so phase alignment holds even under socket jitter.

Every section below is the engineering that makes those three commitments hold inside the 36-hour window.

3. System

3.1 Reasoning layer (System 2)

  • Tavily Search queries the web for instructional content. Query engineering normalises the user's phrase into a search-optimised form ("jab punch" becomes "boxing jab technique biomechanics step by step") so the result set is dominated by structured instruction rather than match commentary.
  • Cohere agent scores the result set for relevance, extracts anatomical landmarks (shoulder rotation, elbow extension, wrist position), maps each landmark to the robot's joint configuration, and cross-references against multiple sources to detect single-source artifacts.
  • Output is a JSON motion guide with phases (preparation, execution, recovery), per-arm joint configurations, timing in milliseconds, velocity profiles, and safety metadata flagging any out-of-envelope joint targets.
  • Multi-stage filtering pipeline: relevance scoring, kinematic-language extraction, cross-source agreement, feasibility check against robot constraints. Roughly 40% of first-pass guides fail at least one stage; the survivors are what reach the compiler.
  • Ablation: dropping the cross-source agreement step alone brings invalid-guide rate back up to roughly 20%. The composition matters more than any single filter.

3.2 Execution layer (System 1)

  • SkillCompiler consumes the JSON motion guide and emits a servo command stream. Inverse kinematics resolves joint targets, cubic-spline trajectory smoothing softens phase transitions, velocity profiling matches the guide's per-phase timing, and safety clamping enforces 0 to 180 degree servo limits and a capped angular velocity.
  • No-overlap zones between the two arms are encoded as forbidden joint-space regions; any trajectory that crosses one is rejected before transmission.
  • RobotControlGenerator serialises the command stream into a binary protocol for the ESP32. Batched updates pack four servo positions per message at 15 Hz, which delivers the equivalent of 60 Hz per-servo control with a fifth of the wire traffic.
  • ESP32 firmware receives commands over Socket.IO, drives the six servos at microsecond timing precision, and sends timestamped acknowledgements back to the host so the visualisation can stay in lockstep.
  • Ablation: removing simulation rehearsal alone brings the unsafe-motion rate back up to roughly 8%. The combination of static safety clamping and dynamic rehearsal is what reaches the under-2% number.

3.3 Frontend and visualisation

  • Next.js + React chat interface renders the reasoning trace live as the LLM streams its motion guide.
  • Three.js 3D viewer renders predicted servo positions in blue against confirmed-from-hardware positions in green. Visual drift between the two surfaces becomes an immediate diagnostic for command-protocol bugs.
  • Arduino UNO + TFT display shows an animated monkey avatar tied to robot state: idle, active, error. Cosmetic on the surface, useful in practice as an at-a-glance status signal on the demo floor.

3.4 Synchronization

  • Timestamp-based command protocol with explicit acknowledgement flow. Drift between visualisation and hardware dropped from 2 to 3 seconds on the first naive implementation to under 50 ms after the protocol redesign.
  • Socket.IO heartbeats every 500 ms. Three consecutive failures trigger a reconnect; the ESP32 enters a known safe state on connection loss rather than holding the last commanded position.
  • Priority queue for emergency-stop and position-reset commands. These bypass the standard batched stream so a safety-critical signal cannot be queued behind routine updates.

4. Evaluation

Evaluation is qualitative and demo-driven, in keeping with what a 36-hour hackathon ship can validate. Numbers below are measured on the build's own protocol, on the four motions exercised during the live demo, not against any external benchmark for natural-language robotic control.

  • Live demo at Hack the North 2025. The robot learned and executed jab punch, wave, beckon, and a celebratory arm raise on the spot, from natural-language input, with no previous training on any of them.
  • 10 to 15 seconds end to end from command to motion across all four demonstrated skills.
  • Invalid motion guides: 40% to under 5% after the four-stage filtering pipeline, measured across the broader set of motions tested during build (not just the four demoed).
  • Unsafe motions: 15% to under 2% after the static safety clamping plus simulation rehearsal pass.
  • Under 50 ms sync between visualiser and hardware, validated by the timestamped acknowledgement flow.

The numbers above are honest about the harness. The hallucination-rate and unsafe-motion-rate improvements are measured on the build's own pipeline against its own first-pass baseline, not against a published natural-language robotic-control benchmark, of which there is no widely accepted equivalent for hackathon-scale dual-arm setups. The four demonstrated skills are an existence proof of the end-to-end loop, not a generalisation claim across the space of all possible motor skills. Read the numbers as evidence that the dual-system architecture closes the text-to-motor loop under hackathon constraints, not as a deployable robotic-control system.

5. Conclusion and Implications

The internet contains the knowledge robots need to learn new tasks. The pipeline that gets it onto silicon is the contribution. The dual-system framing (slow reasoning over messy text, fast execution over precise actuators) generalises beyond hackathon hardware to settings where the bottleneck is not motor precision but reprogramming overhead: assistive healthcare robotics that needs to adapt to a new patient's range of motion in minutes, surgical training rigs that need to demonstrate a new technique without bespoke RL, industrial prototyping where the cost of teaching a new task is the cost of writing a sentence. In each case the safety-critical consequence is the same: a robot that learns from prose, with hallucination filtering and trajectory rehearsal as first-class layers, is the only kind that can be trusted to act on text it has never seen before.

5.1 Limitations

  • Open-loop execution. The robot executes the compiled trajectory without vision feedback on whether the motion is succeeding. A slipped servo or a mechanical obstruction is not detected until the acknowledgement timeout, not from the world.
  • No persistent skill library. Each command relearns the motion from scratch. Two requests for "the jab punch" hit the web twice and re-run the LLM twice. Caching successful guides was scoped out for the hackathon window.
  • Single-source LLM and single-source search. Cohere and Tavily respectively. Provider outages take the system offline. The architecture supports fallback, but fallback was not wired up during the event.
  • Demo-set motion coverage. The four demonstrated motions (jab, wave, beckon, arm raise) are upper-body bilateral motions inside the arms' kinematic envelope. Fine motor skills and motions that require torso or leg coordination are outside what the current 3-DOF arms can express.
  • No closed-loop force or contact sensing. The hand has no end-effector force feedback, so motions that should react to contact (grasping, pushing against resistance) cannot adapt in flight.

5.2 Future Work

  • Vision feedback for closed-loop control. A camera observes the motion, a vision-language model scores how well the executed trajectory matches the requested skill, and the compiler iterates. "Pick up the red cup" becomes the first natural test case.
  • Multi-modal learning. Pose estimation on video demonstrations lets the system learn from a clip rather than a paragraph, which carries more information about phase timing than text can.
  • Persistent skill library with composition. Cache successful motion guides under a stable name. Compose new skills from old ones ("wave plus forward step") rather than re-querying the web.
  • Multi-robot coordination. The same dual-system framing extended across multiple robots that share a command bus, so a single natural-language instruction can drive synchronised motion across more than two arms.
  • Provider fallback. Search and reasoning routed through more than one provider, with automatic failover, so a Cohere or Tavily outage does not take the demo down.
