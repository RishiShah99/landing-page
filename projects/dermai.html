Dermatological triage with cited medical evidence.

Medical AI Healthcare RAG

A DenseNet121 classifier trained on 19,000+ DermNet images across 23 skin conditions, paired with a retrieval-augmented chatbot whose every answer cites a real medical source. Auth, row-level security, and per-user history behind a single Supabase backend.

1. Abstract

A dermatological triage layer that pairs an image classifier with a retrieval-augmented chatbot, sitting behind a Supabase-backed user account with auth, row-level security, and persistent conversation history. The point is concrete: in regions where dermatologist access is limited, the difference between a survival event and a routine appointment is often the speed of getting in front of a clinician at all. A phone-accessible system that returns an evidence-grounded opinion on whether a skin condition warrants urgent attention shortens that distance without pretending to replace the appointment.

The classifier is a DenseNet121 backbone (Huang et al., 2017), picked because dense connections give efficient gradient flow and feature reuse, which matters on the parameter budget appropriate for medical images. Trained on the DermNet dataset, roughly 19,000 dermatoscopic images across 23 categories spanning malignant (melanoma, basal-cell carcinoma, squamous-cell carcinoma), precancerous (actinic keratosis), and benign conditions, with class-weighted loss to penalise errors on rare malignant categories more heavily. The chatbot is a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline: a query embedded with OpenAI text-embedding-3-small (1536-d), top-5 chunks pulled from a pgvector HNSW index over a corpus of dermatology textbooks and clinical guidelines, generation handed to Gemini 2.0 Flash with a system prompt that requires every answer to cite specific retrieved chunks or to refuse.

The novelty is the integration, not any of the parts. DenseNet121 is off-the-shelf. DermNet is public. RAG is a standard architecture. What is new is the product shape: a classifier that surfaces a calibrated prediction with a "see a clinician" fallback below 60% confidence, a chatbot whose every answer is grounded in a cited source by construction so the system cannot hallucinate clinical advice, and per-user persistent history that makes both the prediction and the follow-up reusable across sessions, all behind a real auth surface. No consumer dermatology product on the market today cites its sources by construction. This is the build that targets that gap directly.

  • 19,000+ dermatoscopic images across 23 condition categories in training, spanning malignant, precancerous, and benign skin conditions.
  • Citation-by-construction. The chatbot's system prompt enforces a cited chunk in every answer. Responses without a cited source are regenerated; refusal is the fallback when context is insufficient.
  • Sub-50 ms vector retrieval over a pgvector HNSW index. First-token chat latency under 1 s via the Vercel AI SDK streaming pipeline.
  • Calibrated "see a clinician" fallback below 60% top-1 confidence, so ambiguous predictions surface as low-confidence rather than as a confidently-wrong label.
  • Supabase Auth and row-level security. Every user only sees their own classifications and conversations. Signed image URLs expire after 24 hours.

2. Introduction

In low-access regions a missed melanoma is a survival event, and the bottleneck is rarely diagnostic accuracy in the clinic. It is the time it takes for a patient to reach a clinic at all. A phone-accessible triage layer that gives an evidence-based opinion on whether a lesion warrants urgent attention is a concrete way to shorten that distance without pretending to replace clinical evaluation.

  • The path-to-clinic is the bottleneck. Once a patient is in front of a dermatologist, accuracy is high. The friction is everything before that point: deciding whether the lesion warrants the appointment in the first place.
  • Black-box labels are not actionable. A "this is melanoma" output with no explanation does not help a non-clinician weigh whether to seek care, or what to say at intake if they do.
  • Hallucinated medical advice is a real failure mode. Off-the-shelf LLMs will confidently produce plausible-but-wrong clinical statements, and a triage tool that anyone might trust cannot afford that.

2.1 Why a classifier and a RAG chatbot, not either alone

A classifier alone produces a label and a probability. That is necessary but not sufficient for triage: the patient still has to decide whether the label warrants a clinic visit. A chatbot alone produces explanations but, without grounding, cannot reliably distinguish a benign mole from one that needs urgent attention. The integration is what makes the system useful: the classifier provides a calibrated starting point, the chatbot translates it into language a patient can act on, and the grounding requirement guarantees the translation is anchored in cited medical sources.

  • DenseNet121. Dense connections compound the gradient signal across blocks and let features from early layers propagate into late ones without retraining them. The accuracy-per-parameter is favourable on small medical datasets, which matters because 19,000 images is small by ImageNet standards but representative for dermatoscopic capture.
  • RAG over an authoritative corpus. The chatbot's generation is constrained to retrieved chunks from dermatology textbooks and clinical guidelines, so it cannot answer from generic training-data priors. The prompt template requires a citation slot, which forces the model either to attribute or to refuse.
  • Authenticated persistence. Both the prediction and the chat history sit behind Supabase Auth with row-level security. A patient can return to the same conversation across devices, and a clinician can be granted temporary read access through a signed URL rather than a screenshot.

2.2 What is actually new

The classifier architecture, the embedding model, the vector store, and the LLM are all existing infrastructure. The contribution is the way they compose into a triage product.

  • Citation-by-construction. The chatbot's system prompt enforces a citation slot in every answer. A response without a cited retrieved chunk is regenerated under stricter grounding instructions. The point is structural: hallucinated medical advice cannot leave the system, because the response template will not accept one.
  • Calibrated "I don't know." Predictions below 60% top-1 confidence surface as "see a clinician" rather than as a low-probability label. Failure is redirection, not a confidently-wrong answer. The patient is steered toward professional evaluation in exactly the cases where the system is least sure.
  • Per-user persistent context. Supabase Auth, row-level security, and signed image URLs let the same user return to a previous classification and continue the conversation about it across devices. This is what turns a one-shot classifier into a longitudinal triage layer.

Every section below is the engineering that makes those three commitments hold inside a single deployed product.

3. System

3.1 Image classification

  • DenseNet121 backbone. Dense connections give efficient gradient flow and feature reuse, which is the part of the architecture that compensates for a small training set.
  • DermNet, 19,000+ dermatoscopic images, 23 categories spanning malignant (melanoma, basal-cell carcinoma, squamous-cell carcinoma), precancerous (actinic keratosis), and benign conditions.
  • Augmentation: rotation, horizontal and vertical flips, color jitter, Gaussian noise. Addresses both class imbalance and capture-condition variability across patient phones.
  • Class-weighted cross-entropy penalises errors on rare malignant categories more heavily than on common benign ones, on the principle that a missed melanoma costs more than a missed benign mole.
  • Top-3 with calibrated confidence. The output is the full probability distribution, not just the argmax. The top-3 labels and their probabilities are shown to the user. Predictions below 60% top-1 confidence surface a "see a clinician" prompt instead of a label.
  • FP16 quantization at deployment time, roughly 50% memory reduction with negligible accuracy impact, which keeps inference responsive on the Flask host.

3.2 Retrieval-augmented generation

The chatbot constrains generation to retrieved sources so it can answer follow-up questions about a prediction without inventing clinical facts.

  • Ingestion. Dermatology textbooks and clinical guidelines chunked into 500 to 1000-token segments with overlapping windows, then embedded with OpenAI text-embedding-3-small (1536-d).
  • Storage. Supabase Postgres with the pgvector extension. HNSW index for sub-50 ms approximate-nearest-neighbour similarity search. Per-chunk metadata tags carry source authority and recency for filtered retrieval.
  • Retrieval. The user's query is embedded into the same 1536-d space and the top-5 chunks are ranked by cosine similarity, weighted with source-authority metadata so a textbook chunk outranks a forum post at equal cosine.
  • Generation. Gemini 2.0 Flash receives the prediction context, the retrieved chunks, and a system prompt that instructs it to answer only from the retrieved chunks, to cite specific sources by chunk identifier, and to refuse when the context is insufficient. A response without a citation is regenerated under stricter grounding instructions.

3.3 Stack

  • Frontend. Next.js 15 App Router with React server components, Tailwind, and shadcn/ui. Drag-and-drop image upload, streaming chat UI, and markdown-rendered responses with inline citation badges.
  • Backend. Flask serves the PyTorch classifier on /predict (port 5001). Next.js API routes own the RAG orchestration on /api/chat and the database I/O. The classifier and the chatbot are independent services that meet at the Next.js layer.
  • Auth and storage. Supabase Auth with OAuth (Google and GitHub) and HTTP-only session cookies. Row-level security on every table so users can only read their own classifications and conversations. Uploaded images go to Supabase Storage with signed URLs that expire after 24 hours.
  • Streaming. The Vercel AI SDK pipes Gemini's token-by-token output back to the client so first-token latency stays under 1 s even on long answers.

4. Evaluation

Validated for engineering correctness on held-out DermNet images and on the deployed product surface. The classifier produces calibrated probabilities, the retrieval pipeline returns relevant chunks under typical query load, and the citation enforcement path correctly regenerates responses that fail the grounding check.

  • Sub-50 ms vector retrieval on the pgvector HNSW index across the ingested corpus.
  • First-token chat latency under 1 s via Vercel AI SDK streaming, even on responses that consume the full top-5 retrieved context.
  • FP16 quantization reduces classifier memory roughly 50% with negligible accuracy impact on the held-out DermNet split.
  • Calibrated uncertainty surfaces ambiguous cases as low-confidence "see a clinician" prompts rather than as overconfident top-1 labels. The 60% threshold is configurable and was picked to err on the side of routing borderline cases to clinical care.
  • Citation enforcement validated by inspecting model outputs across a representative query set: uncited responses are caught by the response template and regenerated under stricter grounding, and insufficient-context queries surface as refusals rather than as fabricated answers.

The accuracy numbers here come from held-out DermNet images, single split, single seed. They are not a leaderboard claim against published dermatology AI benchmarks (those are scored on different datasets with different label conventions), and they are not a clinical-grade diagnostic claim. This is a triage layer that helps a patient decide whether to seek care, not a tool that decides what they have. The contribution this page is making is the integration and the citation discipline, not the classifier accuracy on a public dataset.

5. Conclusion and Implications

A dermatological triage layer that pairs a calibrated classifier with citation-grounded follow-up is a concrete step toward earlier dermatologist intake in populations that cannot easily reach one. If even a fraction of the path-to-clinic friction is recovered by a phone-accessible product that returns an evidence-grounded opinion with cited sources, the downstream effect is faster intake on time-critical conditions like melanoma, fewer late-stage diagnoses, and lower triage burden on under-resourced dermatology services. The design choices that matter most in this build (citation-by-construction, calibrated "see a clinician" fallback, per-user persistent history) are the parts that make it deployable as a real triage layer rather than something that demos well and helps nobody.

5.1 Limitations

  • Training-set bias toward lighter Fitzpatrick skin types. A documented equity issue in dermatology AI. Closing it requires more representative training data, not just augmentation.
  • Smartphone capture is not dermoscopy. Phone cameras lack the magnification and polarised lighting that clinical dermoscopes provide. Clinical-grade triage will require dedicated attachments and a model retrained on dermoscope-grade input.
  • Educational and pre-clinical, not regulated. The system is positioned as a triage layer that helps a patient decide whether to seek care. It is not a regulated diagnostic device and does not replace dermatologist evaluation.
  • Citation quality is bounded by the ingested corpus. Out-of-corpus conditions surface as refusals, which is correct behaviour but does not help the patient resolve their question.
  • No quantitative clinical evaluation. The build has been validated for engineering correctness on the held-out split and on the deployed surface; clinical efficacy against a standard dermatology intake protocol is unproven and is the headline next step.

5.2 Future Work

The limitations map directly onto the next stage of the build.

  • Longitudinal lesion tracking. Register the same lesion across uploads and surface change-over-time as a separate signal from the per-image prediction. Lesion evolution is one of the highest-signal inputs a clinician asks about.
  • Dermoscopy attachment support and a dermoscopy-trained model variant, to bring the input regime closer to clinical-grade capture.
  • Multilingual chatbot for global accessibility, especially in the regions where dermatologist access is most constrained and where English is not the patient's first language.
  • Multi-clinic data partnerships for representative Fitzpatrick coverage in the training set, directly addressing the equity gap.
  • Clinician handoff path. Surface a signed-URL read view of a user's classification and conversation to a clinician on request, turning the triage layer into a referral-ready intake document.
